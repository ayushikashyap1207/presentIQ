# Posture and Eye Contact in Presentation Coaching

## Power Posture: The Physical Foundation of Presence

Posture is not merely an aesthetic concern — it directly affects vocal quality, perceived confidence, and audience trust. Research in social psychology (notably Amy Cuddy's work on "power poses" and related studies on nonverbal dominance) consistently shows that open, expansive postures correlate with higher perceived competence and confidence, both for the speaker themselves and for observers.

### Shoulder Alignment
Shoulders should be pulled gently back and down — not rigid military posture, but open. Rolled or hunched shoulders compress the chest cavity, restrict lung capacity, and project self-protection or insecurity. A useful cue: imagine a string pulling the crown of your head toward the ceiling; this naturally aligns the shoulders without tension.

### Spine Angle
The spine should be vertical or very slightly forward-leaning (5–10 degrees) toward the audience. A backward lean signals disengagement or defensiveness. A forward lean signals engagement, but beyond 15 degrees it can feel aggressive or uncomfortable for the audience. In seated presentations, avoid the "slump" — lower back supported, spine tall.

### Open Chest
The sternum should face the audience. Turning away, crossing arms over the chest, or angling the torso sideways are all signals the audience reads as defensive, closed-off, or unconfident — even if the speaker feels fine. An open chest also creates the maximum resonant space for the voice.

**The Power Posture Checklist:**
- Feet shoulder-width apart, weight balanced (avoid rocking)
- Knees soft, not locked
- Hips neutral, not tilted forward or back
- Spine elongated, slight forward lean
- Shoulders back and down, chest open
- Head level, chin parallel to the floor

## Common Bad Posture Patterns and What They Signal

| Posture Pattern | What It Signals to the Audience |
|----------------|----------------------------------|
| Hunched shoulders | Anxiety, low confidence, self-protection |
| Arms crossed over chest | Defensiveness, closed-off, disengaged |
| Weight on one hip ("hip-pop") | Casualness, lack of professionalism |
| Constant weight-shifting / rocking | Nervousness, restlessness, lack of groundedness |
| Head jutting forward ("tech neck") | Habitual screen posture; looks submissive on camera |
| Gripping lectern or podium tightly | Fear, need for anchor, lack of freedom |
| Leaning away from audience | Disengagement, discomfort with the topic |

Identifying your default bad posture pattern is the first step — most speakers have one habitual pattern that appears under stress. Video-record yourself presenting to identify yours.

## Eye Contact Rules: Principles for Live Presentations

Eye contact is among the most powerful nonverbal trust signals available to a speaker. It communicates sincerity, authority, and connection simultaneously.

### The 3–5 Second Rule
The foundational guideline for eye contact in presentations: hold eye contact with one person for 3–5 seconds before shifting to another. This duration is long enough to feel genuine and connecting, but not so long as to feel like staring.

Holding for less than 2 seconds reads as scanning — the audience feels unseen, and the speaker appears nervous. Holding for more than 6–7 seconds on one person begins to feel invasive or confrontational.

### The Z-Pattern (or W-Pattern) Scanning Method
Rather than randomly scanning the room or perpetually focusing on the same section (the "lighthouse" mistake), use a deliberate scanning pattern:

**Z-Pattern:** Start at the front-left of the room → move to the front-right → drop diagonally to the back-left → move to the back-right. This creates systematic coverage of the room.

**W-Pattern variation:** Front-left → back-left → centre → back-right → front-right. This is preferred for wider rooms or amphitheatre-style seating.

The goal is to ensure everyone in the room has received direct eye contact at least once during a 10-minute presentation.

### Avoiding Eye Contact Traps
- **The "friend" trap**: Fixating only on familiar, friendly faces — leaving the rest of the audience disconnected.
- **The notes trap**: Reading from notes breaks eye contact chains; use notes only as anchors, never as scripts.
- **The ceiling/floor trap**: Looking up or down when thinking signals unpreparedness. If you need to think, maintain a neutral forward gaze.
- **The screen trap**: Turning to look at your own slides — a common and critical mistake. The audience is in front of you; the slide is for them, not for you.

## Camera-Specific Eye Contact for Video Presentations

Video presentations and remote meetings require a distinct eye contact strategy. The camera lens is the "eyes" of the remote audience — yet most speakers look at the on-screen faces, not the lens.

### The Camera Lens Rule
Speak to the camera lens, not to your own video feed or the faces on the screen. Looking at the faces appears — from the audience's perspective — as looking *down* or *away*, breaking the connection.

**Practical setup tips:**
- Position your camera at eye level or very slightly above (not below — upward-facing angles are unflattering and project weakness).
- Place a sticky note next to or below the camera lens with a reminder dot or smiley face to cue your gaze.
- Reduce your own video to a small thumbnail at the top of the screen near the camera to minimise the gaze offset.

### The 80/20 Rule for Virtual Presentations
Aim for 80% camera-directed eye contact, 20% looking at notes/slides. Brief glances away are natural; sustained eye contact toward the camera builds the equivalent of in-person connection.

### Lighting and Camera Framing
Eye contact effectiveness is amplified when your face is well-lit from the front. Back-lit or side-lit setups create shadows that make eyes less readable, reducing the impact of even perfect camera gaze.

## How Posture Affects Vocal Projection

Posture and voice are inseparable systems. The vocal mechanism — lungs, diaphragm, larynx, resonating chambers — requires specific physical conditions to function optimally:

1. **Lung capacity**: Slumped posture compresses the lungs by up to 30%, reducing the breath support available for sustained phrases and volume.
2. **Resonance**: Open chest posture creates resonance in the sternum and chest cavity, producing a richer, fuller vocal tone. Closed posture constricts these resonating chambers.
3. **Projection**: Sound projects best when the head is level and the chin is parallel to the floor. A dropped chin directs sound downward; a raised chin thins the voice.
4. **Anxiety management**: Power posture triggers hormonal shifts (reduced cortisol, increased testosterone) that directly reduce the physical symptoms of anxiety — including voice tremor and breathiness.

The practical takeaway: before any high-stakes presentation, take 2 minutes in full power posture, combined with diaphragmatic breathing. This primes both the physical instrument and the psychological state for peak performance.
