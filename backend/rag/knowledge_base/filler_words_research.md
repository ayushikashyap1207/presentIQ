# Filler Words Research: Understanding and Reducing Verbal Crutches

## What Are Filler Words?

Filler words are sounds, words, or phrases that speakers insert into speech while thinking, pausing, or transitioning between ideas. The most common filler words in English include:

- **Vocalised pauses**: "um," "uh," "er," "ah"
- **Discourse markers**: "like," "you know," "so," "right," "okay"
- **Hedging expressions**: "basically," "literally," "kind of," "sort of," "I mean"
- **Repetitive affirmations**: "actually," "honestly," "clearly"

Filler words serve a cognitive purpose — they buy the brain time to formulate the next thought. However, when overused, they undermine the speaker's credibility, distract the audience, and signal a lack of preparation. Studies in communication psychology show that listeners associate frequent filler use with nervousness, low expertise, and poor confidence, even when the underlying content is strong.

---

## Research-Backed Frequency Thresholds

The frequency of filler word use is the primary determinant of whether they become a problem. Research from linguistics and communication coaching fields has established the following benchmarks per minute of speech:

| Frequency | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| **< 5 fillers per minute** | Acceptable — falls within normal conversational range; listeners typically don't notice |
| **5–10 fillers per minute** | Noticeable — listeners begin to register a pattern; credibility starts to erode |
| **> 10 fillers per minute** | Distracting — audience attention shifts from content to counting fillers; speaker loses authority |

At the extreme end (15+ per minute), audience comprehension drops measurably because cognitive load is split between parsing content and filtering out noise. Presenters in professional settings should target fewer than 5 filler words per minute as a baseline goal.

It is worth noting that zero fillers is not the target — completely filler-free speech can sound robotic or over-rehearsed. The goal is natural fluency with strategic silence replacing uncontrolled filler.

## Technique 1: Pause Substitution Method

The most effective single technique for reducing filler words is **Pause Substitution**: deliberately replacing every "um" and "uh" with a silent pause.

**How it works:**
1. Record a 2-minute practice speech on any topic.
2. Play it back and note every filler word — mark the timestamp.
3. In your next recording, at each moment you feel the urge to say "um," instead close your mouth and breathe silently for 1–2 seconds.
4. Repeat until the silence feels comfortable.

The key insight is that a deliberate pause *feels* much longer to the speaker than it sounds to the audience. What feels like a 3-second gap is typically perceived by listeners as a confident, thoughtful pause — a hallmark of expert speakers. Silence is powerful; it signals that you are in control of your delivery.

**Daily micro-practice:** Spend 5 minutes each morning narrating any activity (making coffee, describing your commute plan) with the rule: no filler words allowed. The only substitute is a closed-mouth pause.

## Technique 2: Slow-Down Trigger (Linked to WPM)

Filler words spike when speech rate accelerates. When we speak faster than approximately 160 words per minute, the brain's word-retrieval system lags behind, and filler words rush in to fill the gap. The **Slow-Down Trigger** technique embeds a rate check into your delivery system.

**Implementation:**
1. Identify your personal filler trigger words (most people have 1–2 dominant fillers, e.g., "um" and "like").
2. Every time you catch yourself using a trigger filler, treat it as a signal to consciously reduce your pace by 10–15 WPM.
3. Take a breath, drop your shoulder tension, and continue at a slower rate.
4. Over time, the filler word itself becomes the trigger for correction rather than a problem in isolation.

This technique works because filler words and fast speech are causally linked — slowing down reduces the cognitive gap that fillers are filling. For reference, an ideal presentation pace is 130–160 WPM, and staying within that range naturally suppresses filler frequency.

---

## Technique 3: Preparation Density for Transitions

The highest filler-word density in any presentation occurs at **transitions** — moments where the speaker moves from one idea to the next. Cognitive load peaks here because the brain is simultaneously finishing one thought and retrieving the next.

**Preparation Density** is the practice of scripting, memorising, and rehearsing transition sentences specifically:

1. Map every transition in your presentation: "Moving from X to Y."
2. Write a specific, complete transition sentence for each: *"Now that we understand the problem, let's examine the three solutions that research supports."*
3. Rehearse transitions separately from the body content — 10 dedicated transition run-throughs before full rehearsals.
4. In delivery, these pre-loaded transitions fire automatically, eliminating the cognitive gap where fillers breed.

Preparation density also applies to **opening lines** (speakers are most nervous in the first 30 seconds, producing the highest filler rate) and **Q&A responses** (answering with a structured formula: Restate → Answer → Example eliminates the blank-mind filler cascade).

## Daily 2-Week Drill Protocol

Use this progressive protocol to systematically reduce filler word frequency over 14 days:

**Week 1 — Awareness Phase**
- **Days 1–2:** Record 3 minutes of unscripted speech daily. Count fillers. Establish your baseline filler-per-minute rate.
- **Days 3–4:** Repeat recordings. After each recording, listen back and physically tap the table at every filler. Build awareness.
- **Days 5–7:** Begin Pause Substitution. Record 2-minute sessions. Aim to cut baseline filler rate by 30%.

**Week 2 — Correction Phase**
- **Days 8–9:** Apply Slow-Down Trigger in real conversations. Set a subtle wrist-tap reminder for every filler you catch yourself using.
- **Days 10–11:** Practice prepared transitions only. 15 minutes of transition-sentence drilling for an upcoming talk or hypothetical topic.
- **Days 12–13:** Full 5-minute practice speech with all three techniques active. Target: < 5 fillers per minute.
- **Day 14:** Final recording benchmark. Compare to Day 1. Most practitioners see a 50–70% reduction in filler frequency within this two-week cycle.

Consistent daily practice over this protocol rewires the default speech pattern. The pause becomes the new reflex where the filler used to live.
